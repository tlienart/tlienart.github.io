Splitting methods and ADMM

In these notes, I show how some well known methods from numerical linear algebra can be applied to convex optimisation. The aim of these notes is to give an idea for how the following topics intertwine:

  • solving a system of linear equations via iterative methods

  • operator splitting techniques (Gauss-Seidel, Douglas-Rachford, ...),

  • proximal operator,

  • the alternating direction methods of multipliers also known as ADM or ADMM

From convex optimisation to linear algebra

Decomposable problems

In convex optimisation, a large portion of the problems can be written in the form: x† ⁣ ⁣∈ ⁣ ⁣arg⁡min⁡x f(x)+g(x). x^\dagger \quad\!\! \in\quad\!\! \arg\min_x\,\, f(x) + g(x).x†∈argxmin​f(x)+g(x). This includes constrained problems where g≡ιCg\equiv \iota_Cg≡ιC​ for a convex set CCC or penalised problems like the LASSO regression: x† ⁣ ⁣∈ ⁣ ⁣arg⁡min⁡x 12∥Ax−b∥22+λ∥x∥1 x^\dagger \quad\!\! \in\quad\!\!\arg\min_x\,\, \frac12\|Ax-b\|_2^2 + \lambda\|x\|_1 x†∈argxmin​21​∥Ax−b∥22​+λ∥x∥1​

In a similar vein as for the previous notes, the following regularity conditions are usually assumed to hold:

  1. f,g∈Γ0f, g\in \Gamma_0f,g∈Γ0​ , the space of convex, proper, lower semi-continuous functions,

  2. f,gf, gf,g are such that on dom f∩dom g\mathrm{dom}\, f \cap \mathrm{dom}\, gdomf∩domg , ∂(f+g)=∂f+∂g\partial(f+g)=\partial f+\partial g∂(f+g)=∂f+∂g .

As we showed in convex analysis part 1, for x†x^\daggerx† to be a minimiser, it must verify the first order condition, i.e.:

0 ⁣ ⁣∈ ⁣ ⁣(∂f+∂g)(x†), 0 \quad\!\! \in\quad\!\! (\partial f+\partial g)(x^\dagger), 0∈(∂f+∂g)(x†),

the issue being that, in most cases, we don't have a (cheaply available) closed-form expression for the inverse operator (otherwise the problem is trivial).

This issue can in fact be related to the classical problem of solving a linear system of equations:

Ax ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣b Ax \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! b Ax=b

where AAA is invertible but is (for example) too big or too poorly conditioned for its inverse to be computable cheaply and reliably.

Operator splitting methods in linear algebra

One way of attempting to solve (4) without computing the inverse of AAA is to consider a splitting method: a decomposition of AAA into a sum of matrices A=B+CA=B+CA=B+C with desirable properties. The equation (4) can then be written in the form of a fixed-point equation:

Bx ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣b−Cx. Bx \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! b-Cx. Bx=b−Cx.

Assuming that BBB is easier to invert than AAA , we can consider the fixed-point iteration algorithm:

xk+1 ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣B−1(b−Cxk). x_{k+1} \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! B^{-1} (b-Cx_k). xk+1​=B−1(b−Cxk​).

There are two classical examples of this type of splitting:

  1. the Jacobi method, writing A=D+RA=D+RA=D+R with DDD diagonal,

  2. the Gauss-Seidel method, writing A=(L+D)+UA=(L+D)+UA=(L+D)+U with LLL and UUU lower and upper triangular respectively.

Under some conditions, the corresponding fixed-point iterations converge (see also Ortega and Rheinboldt (2000)). For instance if AAA is symmetric, positive definite or if it is diagonally dominant then Gauss-Seidel converges.

DPR splitting

Researchers like Douglas, Peaceman and Rachford studied this in the mid 1950s to solve linear systems arising from the discretisation of systems of partial differential equations (Peaceman and Rachford (1955), Douglas and Rachford (1956)). They came up with what is now known as the Douglas-Rachford splitting and the Peaceman-Rachford splitting.

The context is simple: assume that we have a decomposition A=B+CA=B+CA=B+C where BBB and/or CCC are poorly conditioned or even singular. In that case, one can try to regularise them by writing

A ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣(B+αI)+(C−αI) A \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! (B+\alpha \mathbf I) + (C-\alpha \mathbf I) A=(B+αI)+(C−αI)

for some α>0\alpha>0α>0 which will shift the minimum singular value of BBB and CCC away from zero (and thereby push them towards diagonally dominant matrices). The fixed-point corresponding to this splitting is

(B+αI)x ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣(b−(C−αI)x). (B+\alpha \mathbf I) x \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! (b-(C-\alpha\mathbf I)x). (B+αI)x=(b−(C−αI)x).

Observe that for a suitably large α\alphaα we could also consider the fixed-point derived from (8) where the role of BBB and CCC are swapped. The resulting fixed point equation is equivalent to (8) but the fixed-point iteration is not and the DPR method suggests alternating between both.

It can be proved that this iteration converges provided α\alphaα is sufficiently large. It belongs to a class of method known as alternating direction methods...

From linear algebra back to convex optimisation

Going back to problem (2), we had noted that a minimiser must be in the kernel of (∂f+∂g)(\partial f+\partial g)(∂f+∂g) :

0 ⁣ ⁣∈ ⁣ ⁣(∂f+∂g)(x†). 0 \quad\!\! \in\quad\!\! (\partial f+\partial g)(x^\dagger). 0∈(∂f+∂g)(x†).

Since we've just seen that splitting operators could be a good idea in linear algebra, we could be tempted to apply exactly the same approach here. But in order to do this, we need to consider the inverse of the following two operators: (∂f+αI)(\partial f+\alpha \mathbf I)(∂f+αI) and (∂g+αI)(\partial g+\alpha \mathbf I)(∂g+αI) .

Proximal operators

Proximal operators can be recovered from a number of nice perspectives and are usually attributed to Moreau (see e.g. Moreau (1965)). Here we'll just cover it briefly aiming to define the prox of a function proxf\mathrm{prox}_fproxf​ and show a key result, i.e.: that proxf≡(∂f+I)−1\mathrm{prox}_f \equiv (\partial f+\mathbf I)^{-1}proxf​≡(∂f+I)−1 .

Let xxx and zzz be such that z∈(∂f+I)(x)z \in (\partial f + \mathbf I)(x)z∈(∂f+I)(x) . We are interested in the inverse map or, in other words, in having xxx in terms of zzz . Rearranging the equation note that we have

0 ⁣ ⁣∈ ⁣ ⁣∂f(x)+(x−z). 0 \quad\!\! \in\quad\!\! \partial f(x) + (x-z). 0∈∂f(x)+(x−z).

Observe that the simple linear functional (x−z)(x-z)(x−z) can be re-expressed as the gradient of a squared ℓ2\ell^2ℓ2 -norm:

∂[12∥x−z∥22] ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣x−z. \partial \left[\frac12\|x-z\|_2^2\right] \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! x-z. ∂[21​∥x−z∥22​]=x−z.

Therefore, we can write (14) as

0 ⁣ ⁣∈ ⁣ ⁣∂[f+12∥⋅−z∥22](x). 0 \quad\!\! \in\quad\!\! \partial \left[f + {1\over 2}\|\cdot-z\|_2^2\right] (x). 0∈∂[f+21​∥⋅−z∥22​](x).

This can be interpreted as a first order condition (FOC) and is equivalent to

x ⁣ ⁣∈ ⁣ ⁣arg⁡min⁡u f(u)+12∥u−z∥22 x \quad\!\! \in\quad\!\! \arg\min_u \, f(u)+{1\over 2}\|u-z\|_2^2 x∈argumin​f(u)+21​∥u−z∥22​

which defines the prox of fff .

For a convex function fff , the proximal operator of fff at a point zzz is defined as proxf(z) ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣arg⁡min⁡u f(u)+12∥u−z∥22 \mathrm{prox}_f(z) \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \arg\min_u \, f(u)+\frac12\|u-z\|_2^2 proxf​(z)=argumin​f(u)+21​∥u−z∥22​

Note that (∂f+αI)≡α(∂(α−1f)+I)(\partial f+\alpha \mathbf I) \equiv \alpha (\partial (\alpha^{-1} f)+\mathbf I)(∂f+αI)≡α(∂(α−1f)+I) so that

α−1(∂f+αI)−1 ⁣ ⁣≡ ⁣ ⁣proxα−1f. \alpha^{-1}(\partial f + \alpha \mathbf I)^{-1} \quad\!\! \equiv\quad\!\! \mathrm{prox}_{\alpha^{-1} f}. α−1(∂f+αI)−1≡proxα−1f​.

Note also that if α\alphaα is sufficiently large, then the objective in (17) is strongly-convex and therefore can only have a unique minimiser meaning that proxα−1f\mathrm{prox}_{\alpha^{-1} f}proxα−1f​ is a well-defined function.

Remark: it may look like we just conjured this proximal operator out of the abyss for nothing but it turns out that a proximal operator exists in closed form for a number of important functions. Among the most known examples is the ℓ1\ell^1ℓ1 -norm whose prox is the soft-thresholding operator and the ιC\iota_CιC​ indicator of a convex set whose proximal operator is the orthogonal projection on that set.

ADMM

Hopefully you saw this one coming: if you take DPR2 (12) and simply replace BBB by ∂f\partial f∂f , CCC by ∂g\partial g∂g and pepper with prox\mathrm{prox}prox you get the ADMM (see e.g. Combettes and Pesquet (2011)).

(Alternative direction method of multipliers (ADMM)) the minimisation problem (2) can be tackled with the following elegant iteration: {xk+1= ⁣ ⁣proxγf(zk−uk)zk+1= ⁣ ⁣proxγg(xk+1+uk)uk+1= ⁣ ⁣uk+xk+1−zk+1 \begin{cases} x_{k+1} &=\quad\!\! \mathrm{prox}_{\gamma f}(z_k-u_k)\\ z_{k+1} &=\quad\!\! \mathrm{prox}_{\gamma g}(x_{k+1}+u_k)\\ u_{k+1} &=\quad\!\! u_k + x_{k+1} - z_{k+1} \end{cases} ⎩⎪⎨⎪⎧​xk+1​zk+1​uk+1​​=proxγf​(zk​−uk​)=proxγg​(xk+1​+uk​)=uk​+xk+1​−zk+1​​ which converges provided γ>0\gamma>0γ>0 is small enough.

When is this helpful?: a frequent scenario has fff complex but differentiable and ggg simple but non-differentiable (e.g. ℓ1\ell^1ℓ1 -norm); in that case, the first prox is a differentiable problem that can be (approximately) solved using a simple/cheap first-order method and the second prox exists in closed form. For instance, regularised maximum likelihood estimation or regularised inverse problems typically have this form.
