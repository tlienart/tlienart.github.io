Probabilistic reasoning with kernel embeddings

Following notations in Song et al. (2013), we still consider two random variables XXX and YYY with joint distribution P(X,Y)P(X, Y)P(X,Y) and, additionally, we consider a prior distribution π\piπ on YYY.

Kernel sum rule

The marginal distribution of XXX can be computed by integrating out YYY from the joint density, i.e.:

Q(X) ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣∫P(X∣Y)dπ(Y) ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣EY∼π[P(X∣Y)]. Q(X) \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \int P(X|Y) \mathrm d{\pi}(Y) \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \mathbb E_{Y\sim\pi}[P(X|Y)]. Q(X)=∫P(X∣Y)dπ(Y)=EY∼π​[P(X∣Y)].

Embedding it, we have

μXπ ⁣ ⁣:= ⁣ ⁣EX∼Q[kX] ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣EY∼π[EX∣Y[kX]], \mu_{_X}^\pi \quad\!\! :=\quad\!\! \mathbb E_{X\sim Q}[k_{_X}] \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \mathbb E_{Y\sim \pi}[\mathbb E_{X|Y}[k_{_X}]], μX​π​:=EX∼Q​[kX​​]=EY∼π​[EX∣Y​[kX​​]],

which leads to the kernel sum rule.

Let XXX and YYY denote two random variables and π\piπ a prior on YYY, then the Kernel sum rule reads μXπ ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣CX∣YμYπ \mu_{_X}^\pi \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \mathcal C_{X|Y}\mu^\pi_{_Y} μX​π​=CX∣Y​μY​π​

This is straightforward to prove using the definition of the conditional embedding (see part 1).

μXπ ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣EX∣Y[kY] ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣EY∼π[CX∣YkY]= ⁣ ⁣CX∣YEY∼π[kY] ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣CX∣YμY\begin{aligned} \mu_{_X}^\pi \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \mathbb E_{X|Y}[k_{_Y}] \quad\!\!&=\quad\!\! \mathbb E_{Y\sim \pi}[\mathcal C_{X|Y}k_{_Y}]\\ &=\quad\!\! \mathcal C_{X|Y}\mathbb E_{Y\sim\pi}[k_{_Y}] \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \mathcal C_{X|Y}\mu_{_Y}\end{aligned}μX​π​=EX∣Y​[kY​​]​=EY∼π​[CX∣Y​kY​​]=CX∣Y​EY∼π​[kY​​]=CX∣Y​μY​​​

The kernel sum rule shows that the conditional embedding operator CX∣Y\mathcal C_{X|Y}CX∣Y​ maps the embedding of π(Y)\pi(Y)π(Y) to that of Q(X)Q(X)Q(X).

In practice, an estimator μ^Yπ\hat\mu_{_Y}^\piμ^​Y​π​ is given in the form ∑i=1nαiky~i=Φ~α\sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i k_{\tilde y_i} = \widetilde\Phi \alpha∑i=1n​αi​ky~​i​​=Φα based on samples {y~i}i=1n\{\tilde y_i\}_{i=1}^n{y~​i​}i=1n​. Let's also assume that the conditional embedding operator has been estimated from a sample {(xi,yi)}i=1m\{(x_i,y_i)\}_{i=1}^m{(xi​,yi​)}i=1m​ drawn from the joint distribution with C^X∣Y=Υ(G+λI)−1Φ\widehat\mathcal C_{X|Y}=\Upsilon(G+\lambda I)^{-1}\PhiCX∣Y​=Υ(G+λI)−1Φ where Υ=(kxi)i=1m\Upsilon = (k_{x_i})_{i=1}^mΥ=(kxi​​)i=1m​, Φ=(kyi)i=1m\Phi=(k_{y_i})_{i=1}^mΦ=(kyi​​)i=1m​, Gij=k(yi,yj)G_{ij} = k(y_i,y_j)Gij​=k(yi​,yj​) and G~ij=k(yi,y~j)\widetilde G_{ij} = k(y_i, \tilde y_j)Gij​=k(yi​,y~​j​).

The kernel sum rule in the finite sample case has the following form: μ^Xπ ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣C^X∣Yμ^Yπ ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣Υ(G+λI)−1ΦG~α. \hat\mu_{_X}^\pi \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \widehat\mathcal C_{X|Y}\hat\mu_{_Y}^\pi \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \Upsilon(G+\lambda I)^{-1}\Phi \widetilde G\alpha. μ^​X​π​=CX∣Y​μ^​Y​π​=Υ(G+λI)−1ΦGα.

Kernel chain rule

A joint distribution QQQ can be factorised into a product between conditional and marginal with Q(X,Y)=P(X∣Y)π(Y)Q(X, Y)=P(X|Y)\pi(Y)Q(X,Y)=P(X∣Y)π(Y).

The kernel chain rule reads CXYπ ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣CX∣YCYYπ. \mathcal C^\pi_{XY} \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \mathcal C_{X|Y}\mathcal C^\pi_{YY}. CXYπ​=CX∣Y​CYYπ​.

This is straightforward to prove:

CXYπ ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣E(X,Y)∼Q[kX⊗kY] ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣EY∼π[EX∣Y[kX]⊗kY]= ⁣ ⁣CX∣YEY∼π[kY⊗kY] ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣CX∣YCYYπ.\begin{aligned} \mathcal C^\pi_{XY} \quad\!\!&=\quad\!\! \mathbb E_{(X,Y)\sim Q}[k_{_X}\otimes k_{_Y}] \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \mathbb E_{Y\sim\pi}[\mathbb E_{X|Y}[k_{_X}] \otimes k_{_Y}]\\ &= \quad\!\! \mathcal C_{X|Y}\mathbb E_{Y\sim \pi}[k_Y \otimes k_Y] \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \mathcal C_{X|Y}\mathcal C^\pi_{YY}.\end{aligned}CXYπ​​=E(X,Y)∼Q​[kX​​⊗kY​​]=EY∼π​[EX∣Y​[kX​​]⊗kY​​]=CX∣Y​EY∼π​[kY​⊗kY​]=CX∣Y​CYYπ​.​
The kernel chain rule in the finite sample case has the following form: C^XYπ ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣C^X∣YC^YYπ ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣Υ(G+λI)−1G~diag(α)Φ~t, \widehat{\mathcal C}_{XY}^\pi \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \widehat{\mathcal C}_{X|Y}\widehat{\mathcal C}^\pi_{YY} \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \Upsilon(G+\lambda I)^{-1}\widetilde G\mathrm{diag}(\alpha)\widetilde\Phi^t,CXYπ​=CX∣Y​CYYπ​=Υ(G+λI)−1Gdiag(α)Φt, using C^YYπ=Φ~diag(α)Φ~t\widehat{\mathcal C}^\pi_{YY} = \widetilde\Phi \mathrm{diag}(\alpha)\widetilde\Phi^tCYYπ​=Φdiag(α)Φt and C^X∣Y=Υ(G+λI)−1Φ\widehat{\mathcal C}_{X|Y} = \Upsilon(G+\lambda I)^{-1}\PhiCX∣Y​=Υ(G+λI)−1Φ.

Kernel Bayes rule

A posterior distribution can be expressed in terms of a prior and a likelihood as

Q(Y∣x) ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣P(x∣Y)π(Y)Q(x), Q(Y|x) \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! {P(x|Y)\pi(Y)\over Q(x)}, Q(Y∣x)=Q(x)P(x∣Y)π(Y)​,

where Q(x)Q(x)Q(x) is the relevant normalisation factor. We seek to construct the conditional embedding operator CY∣Xπ\mathcal C^\pi_{Y|X}CY∣Xπ​.

The kernel Bayes rule reads

μY∣xπ ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣CY∣Xπkx ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣CYXπ(CXXπ)−1kx, \mu^\pi_{_Y|x} \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \mathcal C^\pi_{Y|X}k_x \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \mathcal C^\pi_{YX} (\mathcal C^\pi_{XX})^{-1}k_x, μY​∣xπ​=CY∣Xπ​kx​=CYXπ​(CXXπ​)−1kx​, with then CY∣Xπ=CYXπ(CXXπ)−1\mathcal C^\pi_{Y|X} = \mathcal C^\pi_{YX}(\mathcal C^\pi_{XX})^{-1}CY∣Xπ​=CYXπ​(CXXπ​)−1.

Using the sum rule, CXXπ=C(XX)∣YμYπ\mathcal C^\pi_{XX}=\mathcal C_{(XX)|Y}\mu_{_Y}^\piCXXπ​=C(XX)∣Y​μY​π​ and, using the chain rule, CYXπ=(CX∣YCYYπ)t\mathcal C^\pi_{YX}=(\mathcal C_{X|Y}\mathcal C^\pi_{YY})^tCYXπ​=(CX∣Y​CYYπ​)t. The finite sample case can also be obtained (and is a bit messy).

Kernel Bayesian average and posterior decoding

Say we're interested in evaluating the expected value of a function g∈Hg\in \mathcal Hg∈H with respect to the posterior Q(Y∣x)Q(Y|x)Q(Y∣x) or to decode y⋆y^{\star}y⋆ most typical of the posterior. Assume that the embedding μ^Y∣xπ\widehat\mu^{\pi}_{_{Y|x}}μ​Y∣x​π​ is given as ∑i=1nβi(x)ky~i\sum_{i=1}^n \beta_{i}(x)k_{\tilde y_{i}}∑i=1n​βi​(x)ky~​i​​ and g=∑i=1mαikyig=\sum_{i=1}^m\alpha_{i}k_{y_{i}}g=∑i=1m​αi​kyi​​ then

the kernel Bayes average reads

⟨g,μ^Y∣xπ⟩H ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣βtG~α ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣∑ijαiβj(x)k(yi,y~j), \left\langle g,\widehat\mu_{Y|x}^{\pi}\right\rangle_{\mathcal H} \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \beta^{t} \widetilde G \alpha \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \sum_{ij} \alpha_{i}\beta_{j}(x)k(y_{i},\tilde y_{j}), ⟨g,μ​Y∣xπ​⟩H​=βtGα=ij∑​αi​βj​(x)k(yi​,y~​j​),

and the kernel Bayes posterior decoding reads

y⋆ ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣arg⁡min⁡y −2βtG~:y+k(y,y). y^{\star} \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \arg\min_{y} \,\, -2\beta^{t}\widetilde G_{:y}+k(y,y). y⋆=argymin​−2βtG:y​+k(y,y).

The second expression coming from the minimisation min⁡y∥μ^Y∣xπ−ky∥H2\min_{y}\|\widehat \mu^{\pi}_{_{Y|x}}-k_{y}\|_{\mathcal H}^{2}miny​∥μ​Y∣x​π​−ky​∥H2​.

In general, the optimisation problem is difficult to solve. It corresponds to the so-called "pre-image" problem in kernel methods.
