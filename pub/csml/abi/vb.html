Variational Bayes

In VB the starting point is the variational problem where the discrepancy measure that we attempt to minimise is KL(q,p)\mathrm{KL}(q, p)KL(q,p) with ppp the target distribution, qqq the proxy and KL(q,p)=Eq[log⁡q(X)−log⁡p(X)].\begin{array}{c} \mathrm{KL}(q, p) &=& \mathbb E_q[\log q(X) - \log p(X)]. \end{array}KL(q,p)​=​Eq​[logq(X)−logp(X)].​ The KL is a divergence and so verifies KL(q,p)≥0\mathrm{KL}(q, p)\ge 0KL(q,p)≥0 and KL(p,p)=0\mathrm{KL}(p,p)=0KL(p,p)=0 for all p,q∈P(X)p, q\in\mathcal P(\mathcal X)p,q∈P(X) (which is easy to prove). Of course if we try to minimise it without further constraining the space in which candidates qqq belong, we find the trivial answer q⋆=pq^\star = pq⋆=p.

Mean-Field approximation

A way forward is to consider the space of distributions that fully factorise (mean-field approximation): FMF ⁣ ⁣:= ⁣ ⁣{q∣q(x)∝∏i=1dqi(xi)}. \mathcal F_{_{\text{MF}}} \quad\!\! :=\quad\!\! \left\{q \mid q(x)\propto \prod_{i=1}^d q_i(x_i)\right\}. FMF​​:={q∣q(x)∝i=1∏d​qi​(xi​)}. Note that we could relax this a bit by considering the space of distributions that only factorise according to a specific graphical model but that may generally not lead to as nice simplifications as with "naive" MF and typically requires extra assumptions (but it's doable, see e.g. Ghahramani and Beal (2000) or search generalised mean field).

Under q∈FMFq\in\mathcal F_{_{\text{MF}}}q∈FMF​​, the KL decomposes as KL(q,p) ⁣ ⁣= ⁣ ⁣Eq[∑ilog⁡qi(Xi)−log⁡p(X)]+κ \mathrm{KL}(q, p) \quad\!\! =\quad\!\! \mathbb E_q\left[\sum_i \log q_i(X_i) - \log p(X)\right] + \kappa KL(q,p)=Eq​[i∑​logqi​(Xi​)−logp(X)]+κ where κ\kappaκ corresponds to the log of the normalisation constant of qqq and can be ignored in the minimisation.

Convergence and quality

A nice thing about MFVB is that the scheme provably converges in general (thanks to the convergence properties of coordinate descent). This is generally a very appreciated property of any schemes though your next question should be what does it converge to and how good is it?. To which the answer is, it converges to q⋆∈FMFq^\star \in \mathcal F_{_{\text{MF}}}q⋆∈FMF​​ and it can be... quite bad in general.

To see how bad, let's consider a simple 2D Gaussian ppp with zero mean and a dense covariance matrix. MFVB will give you something like q(x1)q(x2)≈p(x1,x2)q(x_1)q(x_2) \approx p(x_1, x_2)q(x1​)q(x2​)≈p(x1​,x2​). So without even doing any of the calculations, you can already see that cross terms in x1x2x_1x_2x1​x2​ will be completely ignored. If the off-diagonal terms of the covariance matrix are large, this can be very bad.

To summarise, MFVB will give a very poor proxy for any target distribution which has strong dependence structure between (some of) its variables. As a result it may severely underestimate the uncertainty of model variables.
